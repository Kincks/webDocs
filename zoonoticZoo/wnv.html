West Nile Virus

Full name:

West Nile virus (WNV)

Type:

Flavivirus

Vectors:

Mosquitoes (especially Culex), birds

Spreads via:

Mosquito bites

Non-vector Hosts:

Humans, horses, other mammals to a lesser degree

Prevention:

Wearing insect repellent, long-sleeved shirts and pants, staying indoors at dawn and dusk, dumping standing water to prevent mosquito breeding, vaccination (if you're a horse)

First discovered in 1937, West Nile virus has been causing infections all over the world after spreading out of West Nile, Uganda. It was found in New York in 1999 and has been spreading around the United States and into Canada rapidly, driven far and wide by the migration of birds and mild winters for mosquitoes. The virus requires both birds and mosquitoes to complete its lifecycle, other hosts are "dead ends."

WNV is not a very deadly disease (for humans), in fact, most of the time it's asymptomatic, but it can cause flu-like symptoms in approximately 20% of those infected. In about 1 case out of every 150 WNV can cause life-threatening encephalitis (brain swelling) and/or menengitis (spinal cord swelling), most likely to occur in the very young and old.
